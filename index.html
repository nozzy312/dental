About

Clinical roots. Human focus.

Irena Hazon is a Registered Dental Hygienist turned coach, consultant, and speaker — licensed since 1996, with three decades in dentistry. Her perspective was formed where it matters most — chairside — and refined through advanced study, including The Pankey Institute, renowned for its philosophy of relationship-based, patient-centered dentistry.

Patients are physically vulnerable, often anxious, with little chance to express themselves freely — and then asked to make important decisions about their health and finances. To Irena, that isn't a challenge. It's an opportunity.

She brings that same blend of clinical fluency and human insight to dental practices: she speaks the team's language, understands the patient's experience, and knows how to close the gap between the two.

Perspectives

Ideas that start conversations

The greatest opportunity in dentistry isn't the procedure.

After 30 years as a dental hygienist and training at the Pankey Institute, I've come to believe the greatest opportunity in dentistry is found in the human connection.

Think about the dynamic. Our patients are physically vulnerable. They're lying back, often anxious, with little opportunity to express themselves freely. Then, at the end of the appointment, we ask them to make important decisions about their health and finances.

When leadership cultivates trust, communication, consistency, and positivity, the entire practice begins to change. Team members feel empowered. Patients feel heard, respected, and safe. Trust deepens, conversations become easier, and treatment decisions become more meaningful.

What fascinates me most is that this transformation rarely stays within the walls of the practice. It radiates outward. Patients return with greater confidence, they refer the people they love, and the culture becomes one where everyone grows together.

The healthiest practices don't simply create beautiful smiles. They create an environment where people genuinely want to come back — and where they can't wait to tell others about the experience.

What if we've been valuing the title instead of the talent?

The difference between a $3,000 day and a $5,000 day is often an exceptional dental assistant. But perhaps the problem begins with the title.

The words dental assistant immediately place someone into a pay category. We attach a market value to the position before asking a far more important question: what value does this person create?

Do you want someone who simply fulfills a job description? Or someone who anticipates your next move, calms an anxious patient without being asked, protects your focus, keeps treatment flowing, and quietly elevates every appointment?

That isn't just assistance. That's partnership. The title may say Dental Assistant, but the contribution can resemble that of a trusted vice president — someone whose judgment, anticipation, and emotional intelligence directly influence the success of the business.

Perhaps the question isn't, "What's the market rate for a dental assistant?" Perhaps it's this: if someone consistently helps transform a $3,000 day into a $5,000 day, what is that partnership truly worth?
